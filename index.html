Research Agenda

My research broadly centers on understanding inter- and intra-group dynamics and their consequences for political attitudes, behavior, and policy preferences. More specifically, I am interested in how racial conceptions of national identity drive a range of attitudes and behavior. My dissertation topic explores the dynamics between national and partisan identities, focusing on two broad questions: (1) Who and what are perceived as threats to American national identity? And (2) What are the attitudinal, behavioral, and policy consequences of a national identity threat from members of (a) an ingroup or (b) an outgroup? This research agenda leverages a mixed-method design and survey experiments to investigate who and what Americans understand as threats to their identity and whether such threats have consequences for political attitudes and behaviors toward deviant outgroup or ingroup members. Using the frameworks of ingroup policing and motivated reasoning, I explore intra-group dynamics when members of a partisan ingroup violate their views of American values and norms. Additionally, I examine the effects of national identity threats on a range of outcomes including vote choice and support for civil restrictions, political threats and harassment, anti-democratic behavior, and political violence. In the future, I plan to expand my research to investigate national identity threats from racial/ethnic outgroups and ingroups. Beyond my dissertation, my research agenda is grounded in my interests in political psychology, affective polarization.
